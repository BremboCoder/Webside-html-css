Morocco is a North African country with beautiful scenery, fascinating history and lots of culture, including delicious cuisine, to explore.

Historic evidence suggests that Morocco has been inhabited by humans since the Paleolithic era of prehistoric times (10.000 BC and beyound). The Maghreb (as the Northern Africa area is called) was a fertile savannah then and not at all like today’s modern arid landscape.

In still ancient, but more recent, history Morocco has been occupied by the Romans, Vandals, Visigoths, and Byzantines. Morocco and the rest of North Africa were drawn into the emerging Mediterranean world by the Phoenicians as they established settlements and trading colonies. The earliest known independent Moroccan kingdom was the King Bocchus I, a Berber of Mauretania.

Al-Andalus (South of spain) is firstly part of the extension of the Umayyad Caliphate, that is to say the medieval Muslim Empire of which it retains the structure. He emancipated himself from it in the tenth century by becoming himself a Caliphate, a period which corresponds to its peak. Ravaged by a civil war (1011-1031), Al-Andalus fragmented into weakened rival kingdoms which were annexed by Christian forces in the north, then conquered in the south successively by the Almoravids and Almohads. If Andalusian culture was reborn several times from these upheavals, from the 12th century, the general picture is that of a slow decline punctuated by the Reconquista which ended with the capture of Granada in 1492.

The conquest and domination of the country by the morrocan were as rapid as they were unforeseen and corresponded to the rise of the Muslim world. Al-Andalus became from the ninth century a center of high culture in medieval Europe, attracting a large number of scholars and thus opening a period of rich cultural flourishing, which earned it the title of “original civilization”.

Sultan Mohammed III established the Moroccan-American Treaty of Friendship in 1786. As the American Revolution began, the country’s Atlantic Ocean merchant ships were being attacked by Barbary pirates. On December 20, 1777, the Sultan declared that all American merchant ships were now under his protection and thus could enjoy safe passage. Therefore, Morocco was the first nation to recognize the newly formed country of the United States formally as an independent nation. This treaty today is the U.S.’s oldest non-broken friendship treaty.
